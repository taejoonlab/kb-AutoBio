# A microwell platform for high-throughput longitudinal phenotyping and selective retrieval of organoids

## Background
- Organoids are powerful experimental models for disease research (especially cancer), but conventional bulk culture makes tracking individual organoid growth difficult
- Local spatial variation in bulk matrices makes it impossible to distinguish whether phenotypic heterogeneity arises from intrinsic cellular differences or microenvironmental differences
- A high-throughput quantitative phenotyping platform at single-organoid resolution with genotype-phenotype mapping capability is needed
- Selective retrieval mechanism should enable molecular profiling of organoids with phenotypes of interest

## Key Experiment Methods
1. **Microwell platform**: Easy-to-manufacture microwell device for single-organoid phenotypic profiling
2. **Longitudinal phenotypic tracking**: Time-course measurement of various phenotypic traits including growth rate, cellular movement, and apical-basal polarity
3. **Selective organoid retrieval**: Physical retrieval of organoids of interest from microwells for single-organoid sequencing
4. **Deep learning image analysis pipeline**: Automated quantification and analysis suite integration for image-based parameters
5. **CRISPR-engineered human gastric organoid models**: Validation of genetic changes and phenotypic associations

## Results
- Successful high-throughput phenotypic profiling at single-cell/organoid resolution
- Identified genomic changes associated with increased growth rate and accessibility/expression changes correlated with apical-basal polarity
- Integrated single-organoid sequencing with physical retrieval mechanism for genotype-phenotype mapping
- Longitudinal tracking enabled quantification of time-course phenotypic changes in individual organoids

## Perspective
- Microwell platforms contribute to organoid-based high-throughput drug screening and disease modeling
- Integration of selective retrieval and single-organoid sequencing is valuable for precision medicine research
- Deep learning-based image analysis will become a standard tool for organoid phenotypic analysis
- Patient-derived organoid cohorts enable personalized treatment prediction through individual response profiling
- Integration of automated imaging and microwell control will transform organoid research paradigms
